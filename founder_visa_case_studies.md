# Founder Visa Case Studies — Immigrant Founder Pathways

> **This is research context, not legal advice.** Immigration outcomes are
> highly fact-specific. Verify everything below with a licensed U.S.
> immigration attorney before acting.

A concise research note on immigrant founders who used, or publicly discussed,
non-green-card-first routes to founding a company in the U.S. — H-1B founder
structures, O-1A, concurrent/self-sponsored H-1B, and similar pathways.

Each profile separates **confirmed facts** (supported by cited sources) from
**reasonable interpretation** and **what is unclear**. Where a fact is not
directly stated by a source, it is labeled "not confirmed" or "unclear."

## Jyoti Bansal — AppDynamics

### Snapshot

- **Country of origin:** India
- **Company:** AppDynamics (application performance monitoring)
- **Founded year:** 2008
- **Role:** Founder & CEO
- **Visa pathway mentioned publicly:** Came to the U.S. on an H-1B (~2000);
  publicly stated he waited ~7 years for a green card and founded the company
  *after* receiving permanent residency.
- **Did they wait for green card before founding?** **Yes** (publicly
  documented — the central theme of his immigration story).
- **Relevance to Clinton's situation:** The canonical "green-card-first"
  cautionary tale. Bansal is the most-cited example of an Indian H-1B holder
  who felt he *could not* start a company until his status was resolved.

### Timeline

- **Year: ~2000** — Immigration/career: Moved to the U.S. on an H-1B at age ~21
  to work at a Silicon Valley startup (reported).
- **Year: ~2000–2007** — Immigration: ~7-year wait for a green card; publicly
  described frustration at being unable to start a company while on H-1B.
- **Year: ~2007–2008** — Immigration: Received green card; reportedly quit his
  job "even before the final paperwork came through."
- **Year: 2008** — Startup: Founded AppDynamics.
- **Year: 2017** — Startup: Cisco acquired AppDynamics for ~$3.7B (reported,
  just before its planned IPO).

### What is publicly known

- He immigrated on an H-1B around 2000 and built his career in Silicon Valley
  (publicly documented in multiple interviews).
- He has repeatedly framed the ~7-year green-card wait as the reason he delayed
  founding a company, stating that on a work visa he was not permitted to start
  his own venture and that he founded AppDynamics once he had a green card
  (reported across multiple secondary profiles).
- AppDynamics was founded in 2008 and acquired by Cisco for ~$3.7B in 2017
  (widely reported).
- He has since become a serial founder/investor (e.g., Harness, Traceable,
  Unusual Ventures) and an outspoken commentator on high-skilled immigration.

### What is unclear

- **Exact visa status at founding:** Reported as green card / permanent
  resident, but the precise category (EB-2/EB-3) and exact approval date are
  not confirmed in primary sources reviewed here.
- **Whether his then-employer sponsored him:** Presumed employer-sponsored
  green card, but not confirmed in the sources here.
- **Exact transition year:** The "~7 years" and 2007–2008 window are reported
  approximations, not precisely documented dates.
- Whether he *legally had to* wait vs. *chose* to wait is a matter of
  interpretation. His public framing is that founding on H-1B was not viable
  for him; the modern legal view (see "Lessons" below) is more nuanced.

### Lessons for an Indian H-1B founder

Bansal's story is from the 2000s and predates wider awareness of structures
like concurrent H-1B and founder-as-beneficiary arrangements. The *modern*
takeaway is **not** "you must wait for a green card." It is: (1) on H-1B you
generally cannot simply work for your own company without a valid
employer-employee/sponsorship structure, and (2) the green-card backlog for
Indian nationals can be a decade-plus, so waiting may not be a realistic plan.
His experience illustrates the *cost of waiting*, which motivates exploring the
O-1A / concurrent-H-1B / founder-H-1B routes the other founders below used.

## Arko Chattopadhyay — Pipeshift

### Snapshot

- **Country of origin:** India
- **Company:** Pipeshift (AI infrastructure / orchestration for open-source GenAI models)
- **Founded year:** 2024
- **Role:** Co-founder & CEO
- **Visa pathway mentioned publicly:** Reported to be on an **O-1 visa**, with
  the petition funded/supported via Y Combinator. (Co-founders reported to be
  on O-1 as well.)
- **Did they wait for green card before founding?** **No** (reported — founded
  on O-1, not a green card).
- **Relevance to Clinton's situation:** A current, AI-startup example of an
  Indian founder using **O-1** rather than waiting for a green card — the most
  directly comparable modern path for "start now, on a non-immigrant visa."

### Timeline

- **Year: 2024** — Startup: Pipeshift co-founded by Arko Chattopadhyay, Enrique
  Ferrao, and Pranav Reddy; admitted to Y Combinator (S24 batch).
- **Year: 2024** — Immigration: Reported that the founders are in the U.S. on
  O-1 visas, with YC funding the petition (reported).
- **Year: Jan 2025** — Startup: Raised ~$2.5M seed led by Y Combinator and
  SenseAI Ventures (reported).
- **Year: 2025** — Recognition: Forbes 30 Under 30 Asia (per his own LinkedIn
  references).

### What is publicly known

- Pipeshift is a YC-backed (S24) AI-infrastructure company founded in 2024,
  operating across San Francisco and Bengaluru (reported).
- It raised ~$2.5M in seed funding in early 2025 (reported).
- A business-press article reported that Arko and his co-founders are on **O-1
  visas funded by Y Combinator** (reported/secondary).

### What is unclear

- **Exact visa status at founding:** Reported as O-1; the exact approval date
  and whether he held any other status (e.g., F-1/OPT, prior H-1B) immediately
  before is not confirmed here.
- **Whether the company sponsored him:** O-1 petitions require a U.S. petitioner
  /agent; whether Pipeshift itself, an agent, or another entity served as
  petitioner is not confirmed.
- **Exact transition year:** Not precisely documented.
- Whether all three co-founders are on identical statuses is reported but not
  individually confirmed.

### Lessons for an Indian H-1B founder

A live proof point that an Indian AI founder can build a venture-backed company
on **O-1A** without waiting for a green card. Note the enabling context: a YC
accelerator that helps structure and fund the O-1 petition. The O-1 "extraordinary
ability" bar is real — it typically requires a documented record (press, awards,
funding, judging, memberships). For someone with PERM already approved, an
approved PERM/I-140 can *itself* become evidence toward O-1A or EB-1A.

## Soundarya Balasubramani — Founder / O-1A case (Unshackled)

### Snapshot

- **Country of origin:** India
- **Company:** Unshackled (author/creator business; community "unshackled.club"
  and the book *Unshackled*)
- **Founded year:** ~2022–2023 (built her venture after leaving corporate roles)
- **Role:** Founder, author, creator
- **Visa pathway mentioned publicly:** Came on F-1 (Columbia, MS 2019),
  worked on H-1B/employment visas, then obtained an **O-1A** as a **solo
  founder** (publicly documented; she writes extensively about it).
- **Did they wait for green card before founding?** **No** (publicly
  documented — O-1A as a solo founder, not a green card).
- **Relevance to Clinton's situation:** The clearest *first-person, documented*
  account of an Indian national self-directing an O-1A as a solo founder — and
  she has published tactical guidance (the book *Unshackled*, co-authored with
  an immigration attorney) on exactly the H-1B → O-1 → EB-1A path.

### Timeline

- **Year: 2019** — Immigration/career: Completed MS at Columbia University;
  later worked (incl. PM at Salesforce) on employment-based status.
- **Year: Dec 2022** — Immigration/career: Reported she returned to India to
  build her own company.
- **Year: 2023 (by ~Sept 2023)** — Immigration: **O-1A approved as a solo
  founder** (publicly documented by her).
- **Year: Aug 2023** — Startup: Launched unshackled.club; *Unshackled* book
  (co-authored with immigration lawyer Sameer Khedekar), funded via
  crowdfunding + Emergent Ventures India grant.

### What is publicly known

- She is an Indian immigrant, Columbia MS (2019), former PM at Salesforce
  (publicly documented on her LinkedIn/site).
- She publicly documented obtaining an **O-1A visa as a solo founder** (~2023),
  and writes openly about the O-1 / EB-1A / H-1B landscape.
- She authored *Unshackled: A Practical Guide for Highly-Skilled Immigrants*
  with U.S. immigration attorney Sameer Khedekar, and runs the unshackled.club
  community (publicly documented).

### What is unclear

- **Exact visa status immediately before O-1A** (H-1B vs. other) and the precise
  filing/approval dates are described by her in posts but not exhaustively
  confirmed in the sources reviewed here.
- **Who served as O-1 petitioner/agent** for the solo-founder O-1A is not
  detailed in the sources here.
- **Exact transition year** from employment-based status to founder status is
  approximate (2022–2023).

### Lessons for an Indian H-1B founder

She is, in effect, a *living playbook* for the O-1 → EB-1A founder route, and
her book is co-written with an immigration attorney — a high-signal secondary
resource (though still not a substitute for your own counsel). Practical
takeaway: the O-1A "solo founder" structure is real and achievable for Indian
nationals, but it requires building and *documenting* an extraordinary-ability
record. Her work is also a useful map of how H-1B, O-1, and EB-1A interrelate.

# Round 2 — More Indian Founders: H-1B / No-Visa → U.S. Startup Pathways

This second set focuses on Indian founders who moved from **H-1B (or student/no
work visa) toward a founder-friendly status** — O-1A, EB-1A, the International
Entrepreneur Rule ("startup parole"), or who were *blocked* by the system and
became cautionary tales. Same evidentiary discipline: confirmed facts are

## Srikanth Narayan — Cache (Cache Financials)

> The single most on-point example for "no green card yet → U.S. **startup
> visa**." He used the **International Entrepreneur Rule (IER / startup parole)**
> — the closest thing the U.S. has to a dedicated startup visa.

### Snapshot

- **Country of origin:** India
- **Company:** Cache Financials (fintech; modern "exchange fund" for concentrated stock)
- **Founded year:** ~2022–2023 (first product launched March 2024)
- **Role:** Founder & CEO
- **Visa pathway mentioned publicly:** Spent ~a decade on the green-card track
  (employment-based) while at Big Tech (Uber, Waymo); then obtained
  **International Entrepreneur Parole (IEP/IER)** to leave his job and build
  Cache — reportedly one of the few approvals after the program was revived.
- **Did they wait for green card before founding?** **No** — he explicitly chose
  *not* to keep waiting and used startup parole instead (publicly documented).
- **Relevance to Clinton's situation:** Direct proof that the **"startup visa"
  (IER parole)** can free an Indian national stuck in the green-card backlog to
  found a venture-scale company — without an O-1A and without a green card.

### Timeline

- **Year: ~2010s** — Career/immigration: Senior engineer/leader at Uber and
  Waymo; ~a decade on the employment-based green-card track (reported).
- **Year: ~2022–2023** — Immigration: Worked with immigration attorney Seth
  Leech; **IER parole approved** to pursue his startup (reported).
- **Year: ~2023** — Startup: Founded Cache Financials.
- **Year: Mar 2024** — Startup: Launched first product (exchange-fund platform).

### What is publicly known

- Ex-Uber/Waymo engineer-leader who left Big Tech to found Cache (reported).
- He pursued **International Entrepreneur Parole** rather than continuing to wait
  on the green card, and was cited as an early/rare approval after the program
  was revived (reported, including a first-person LinkedIn essay).
- Cache launched its inaugural exchange-fund product in March 2024 (reported).

### What is unclear

- **Exact prior visa status** (H-1B vs. other) before IER is not fully confirmed
  here, though a long employment-based green-card wait is reported.
- **Exact IER approval date** and parole validity window are not confirmed here.
- Whether he has since layered on another status (O-1A/EB-1A) is not confirmed.

### Lessons for an Indian H-1B founder

IER parole is real and has been used successfully by an Indian founder in
exactly the "stuck in the backlog" position. Caveats: IER is *parole*, not a
visa or a path to a green card; approvals are few (historically a small number
out of all applications); it requires a qualifying startup (substantial U.S.
investor funding or grants, ownership ≥10%, central/active role) and skilled
legal strategy. Good "build-now" option if you can raise qualifying capital.

## The U.S. "Startup Visa": International Entrepreneur Rule (IER)

The user's question references a "startup visa." Important clarification: **the
U.S. has no dedicated startup visa.** The closest mechanism is the **International
Entrepreneur Rule (IER)**, which grants **parole** (not a visa, not a green card)
to qualifying founders. Srikanth Narayan (above) is a documented Indian user.

**Confirmed facts (per USCIS):**

- IER lets DHS grant **parole** to entrepreneurs for an initial period of up to
  **30 months**, extendable by up to **30 more** (5 years total).
- Core eligibility (high level): you generally must **own at least 10%** of a
  recently-formed U.S. startup, have a **central and active role**, and show the
  startup has **substantial potential for rapid growth and job creation** —
  typically evidenced by **≥ ~$311K in qualified U.S. investor funding**, **≥
  ~$124K in qualified government grants**, or partial-plus-other evidence
  (thresholds are periodically adjusted for inflation; verify current numbers).

**Reasonable interpretation / caveats:**

- It is **parole**, which is **discretionary and revocable**, and does **not**
  directly lead to a green card. Approvals have historically been **few**.
- It can let a backlogged Indian founder **build in the U.S. now**, but it's
  usually paired with a longer-term plan (O-1A → EB-1A).

**Sources:** [USCIS — International Entrepreneur Rule](https://www.uscis.gov/working-in-the-united-states/international-entrepreneur-rule) *(primary/government)* · [Boundless — IER guide](https://www.boundless.com/immigration-resources/international-entrepreneur-rule) *(secondary)*

## Practical Paths for Clinton

Context assumed: **Indian national, currently on H-1B, PERM approved**
(I-140 not necessarily filed/approved yet), wants to start an **AI/software**
startup **before** receiving a green card. Indian EB-2/EB-3 priority dates are
backlogged many years, so "just wait for the green card" may mean a decade-plus.

> **Reminder: research context, not legal advice.** "PERM approved" is an early
> milestone — it is *not* an I-140 approval and does not by itself confer any
> ability to work for a new company. Confirm your exact stage with counsel.

### 1. Stay on current H-1B + own equity only

- **What it allows:** You can *own* equity in a startup and be a passive
  shareholder/investor while remaining employed (and sponsored) by your current
  H-1B employer. You can incorporate, hold founder shares, and let the company
  exist.
- **What it does not allow:** You generally **cannot actively work** for or
  perform services for your own startup on the current H-1B (it authorizes work
  only for the sponsoring employer in the approved role). No operational/CEO
  work, no "moonlighting" for the new entity.
- **When it becomes realistic:** Immediately — but only as a *passive owner*
  while a co-founder/team runs operations.
- **Risk level:** **Low** (if truly passive and well-documented) — but easy to
  cross the line into unauthorized work.
- **Attorney questions to ask:** What exactly counts as "passive" ownership vs.
  prohibited "work"? Can I be a board member, sign incorporation docs, or make
  high-level decisions? How do I keep my primary H-1B clean while the company
  exists?

### 2. Concurrent H-1B through the startup

- **What it allows:** A *second*, concurrent H-1B filed by the startup (or a
  friendly entity) lets you work for both your current employer **and** the
  startup at the same time, each part-time, on cap-exempt concurrent petitions
  (you already hold an H-1B, so no new lottery needed).
- **What it does not allow:** The startup petition still needs a valid
  employer-employee relationship and a real role/wage; you can't simply "pay
  yourself nothing." It doesn't remove your dependence on maintaining the
  primary H-1B.
- **When it becomes realistic:** Once the startup is a real entity that can act
  as an employer (EIN, ability to pay required wage, governance to supervise
  you). Often feasible relatively early.
- **Risk level:** **Medium** — structurally sound and common, but documentation
  and the employer-employee relationship must be solid.
- **Attorney questions to ask:** Can my startup file a concurrent H-1B for me
  now? What governance (independent board / control) do I need so the company
  can lawfully "employ" me? What's the minimum viable wage/role? What happens to
  the concurrent H-1B if my primary H-1B ends?

### 3. Founder H-1B / beneficiary-owner route (Sid Sarasvati-style)

- **What it allows:** Under the 2025 H-1B modernization rules, a founder who
  **owns a controlling interest** can be the H-1B *beneficiary* of their own
  company if there's a bona fide employer-employee relationship (e.g., an
  independent board with authority to hire/fire/supervise the beneficiary).
  Your startup sponsors *you*. (Initial/extension validity for owner-founders
  may be limited to shorter increments.)
- **What it does not allow:** You can't be the sole, unchecked controller with
  no oversight structure; the relationship must be genuine. It's more legally
  delicate than a standard third-party H-1B. A *receipt* is not an approval.
- **When it becomes realistic:** Once you've built the corporate governance
  (board/control terms) and the company can pay the required wage. Often after
  some traction/funding.
- **Risk level:** **Medium-High** — newer, scrutinized structure; viable but
  must be set up precisely.
- **Attorney questions to ask:** Does my cap structure/board satisfy the
  beneficiary-owner employer-employee test? What validity period should I
  expect? How does this interact with my existing PERM/priority date? What are
  the renewal risks?

### 4. O-1A founder route (Arko / Soundarya / Aditya-style)

- **What it allows:** O-1A ("extraordinary ability") lets you work for your own
  venture (via a U.S. petitioner/agent) without the H-1B lottery or a green
  card. No annual cap; renewable. Lets you run the company actively.
- **What it does not allow:** It's not a green card and is tied to qualifying
  activity; you must *meet the extraordinary-ability bar* (multiple criteria:
  press, awards, funding, judging, memberships, original contributions). It
  needs a petitioner/agent structure.
- **When it becomes realistic:** Once you've **built and documented** a
  qualifying record. Your **approved PERM** (and any future I-140) can be *one*
  supporting data point. Realistic in months-to-a-year-plus of deliberate
  record-building.
- **Risk level:** **Medium** — well-trodden founder path, but approval depends
  on evidence quality.
- **Attorney questions to ask:** Given my record, which O-1A criteria do I
  plausibly meet today, and what gaps should I close? Who serves as my
  petitioner/agent as a founder? Can I move H-1B → O-1A without leaving the
  U.S.? Does pursuing O-1A jeopardize my existing PERM/green-card track?

### 5. EB-1A long-term route (the green-card version of O-1A)

- **What it allows:** EB-1A is a *self-petitioned green card* for extraordinary
  ability — **no employer sponsor and no PERM needed**, and (critically for
  Indian nationals) it can have a **much shorter backlog** than EB-2/EB-3.
  Aditya Sharma's documented path: O-1 to operate, EB-1A for the green card.
- **What it does not allow:** It's a high bar (similar criteria to O-1A but
  judged more strictly) and doesn't itself authorize work while pending (you
  still need an underlying status like H-1B/O-1).
- **When it becomes realistic:** After building a strong, *sustained* record —
  typically the medium-to-long term, often layered *on top of* an O-1A you got
  first.
- **Risk level:** **Medium-High** (on evidence/approval) but **strategically
  high-value** because it can leapfrog the EB-2/EB-3 India backlog.
- **Attorney questions to ask:** Is EB-1A realistic for me and on what timeline?
  Should I file EB-1A while keeping my existing EB-2/EB-3 (PERM) case alive for
  priority-date retention? Can I "port" my current priority date to an EB-1A
  I-140? O-1A first, then EB-1A — right sequence for me?

### 6. Wait for I-140 / EAD / green card path (the Jyoti-Bansal default)

- **What it allows:** Stay on H-1B with your employer, let PERM → I-140 →
  (eventually) I-485/EAD play out. With an approved I-140 you get H-1B
  extensions beyond 6 years; once you can file I-485, you'd get an **EAD** that
  *does* let you work for your own company.
- **What it does not allow:** Until you reach the I-485/EAD stage, you **cannot
  actively work** for your own startup. For Indian EB-2/EB-3, reaching I-485 can
  take **many years** due to backlogs — so the EAD that unlocks founding may be
  far away.
- **When it becomes realistic:** EAD eligibility depends on your priority date
  becoming "current" (or being filed under any available window) — potentially
  a decade-plus for India EB-2/EB-3. I-140 approval (sooner) only buys H-1B
  extensions, not the right to work for the startup.
- **Risk level:** **Low immigration risk**, but **high opportunity cost** —
  this is precisely the "waited 7 years" scenario that delayed Jyoti Bansal.
- **Attorney questions to ask:** What's my realistic I-485/EAD timeline given my
  category and priority date? Does an approved I-140 let me do *anything* with my
  own company before I-485? Can I run an O-1A/EB-1A track *in parallel* without
  losing my current priority date?

### 7. International Entrepreneur Rule (IER / "startup parole") — Srikanth Narayan-style

- **What it allows:** DHS parole to live in the U.S. and run your startup for up
  to 30 months (+30 month extension). No green card and no O-1 needed; designed
  specifically for high-growth founders. Lets you actively run the company.
- **What it does not allow:** It's **parole, not a visa** — discretionary,
  revocable, and **not** a path to a green card by itself. Requires a qualifying
  startup: ~≥10% ownership, central/active role, and substantial U.S. investor
  funding (~$311K+) or qualified grants (~$124K+) — so it's gated on raising real
  money. Historically few approvals.
- **When it becomes realistic:** Once you've formed the startup *and* raised
  qualifying U.S. investor/grant funding (i.e., not at day-zero idea stage).
- **Risk level:** **Medium-High** — politically/administratively sensitive,
  revocable, and approval rates are low; needs strong legal strategy.
- **Attorney questions to ask:** Do I meet the funding/ownership thresholds (and
  what are the current numbers)? How does parole interact with my existing H-1B
  and PERM/priority date — do I have to abandon anything? What's my bridge from
  IER parole to a durable status (O-1A/EB-1A) before it expires?

### Quick synthesis for Clinton

- **Want to start building now, passively:** Path 1 (own equity, co-founder
  runs ops).
- **Want to actively work on the startup soonest while keeping H-1B:** Paths 2
  or 3 (concurrent / founder H-1B).
- **Want to fully run the company on your own status:** Path 4 (O-1A) — the
  route all three *modern* founders above used or discussed.
- **Want to escape the India backlog for the green card itself:** Path 5
  (EB-1A), ideally layered on an O-1A.
- **Already raising real U.S. funding and want to relocate/build now:** Path 7
  (IER startup parole) — the literal "startup visa," used successfully by an
  Indian founder (Srikanth Narayan), but gated on funding and revocable.
- **Lowest effort, highest opportunity cost:** Path 6 (wait) — the dated
  default; rarely the best plan for an Indian national given backlog length.

These paths are **not mutually exclusive** — a common strategy is *concurrent/
founder H-1B or O-1A to build now* **+** *EB-1A in parallel to fix the green
card*, while keeping the existing PERM/EB-2 case alive for priority-date
retention. For genuinely unconventional options, see **Round 3** below.

### Snapshot

### Lessons for an Indian H-1B founder

EB-1A is **self-petitioned** (no employer, no PERM) and the "extraordinary
ability" record can be built from *founder* activities — awards, speaking,
press, judging, original contributions — not only research papers. If your
spouse is the H-1B holder and you're on H-4, the H-4 EAD lets you *operate*, and
a self-won EB-1A can flip the whole household's status. For Clinton: an approved
PERM is one data point, but EB-1A wants a *sustained, documented* record.

## Five non-traditional *mechanisms* (with real-world usage)

### A. E-2 via a second citizenship (Grenada / Turkey) — "passport arbitrage"

- **The hack:** India has **no E-2 treaty**, so Indians are normally locked out
  of the investor-friendly E-2 visa. Some founders obtain **citizenship-by-
  investment** (e.g., **Grenada** ~$200K+ donation, or Turkey) — an E-2 treaty
  country — *then* qualify for an **E-2** to run a U.S. business.
- **What it allows:** Live in the U.S. and actively run your company while you
  invest a "substantial" amount; renewable indefinitely while the business operates.
- **What it does not allow / caveats:** E-2 is **not** a direct green-card path;
  it's tied to the business. The **AMIGOS Act** added a **~3-year domicile
  requirement** for citizenship-by-investment E-2 applicants — diligence required.
  Real money for the second passport *plus* the U.S. investment.
- **Real-world use:** Documented as a recurring strategy among Indian entrepreneurs
  (multiple immigration-law firms describe named-anonymous client cases).
- **Sources:** [Malescu Law — E-2 via Grenada CBI](https://malesculaw.com/get-us-e-2-visa-with-grenada-citizenship-by-investment/) · [Ashoori Law — E-2 for India](https://www.ashoorilaw.com/blog/e2-visa-india/) *(both secondary/law-firm)*

### B. The "flip": found abroad first → **L-1 "new office"** → **EB-1C** green card

- **The hack:** Start the company **in India**, run it ≥1 year as a manager/
  executive, open a **U.S. subsidiary/affiliate**, then transfer *yourself* in on
  an **L-1A "new office"** visa. Later self-route to the **EB-1C** multinational-
  manager green card — **no PERM**, and often a **shorter India backlog** than
  EB-2/EB-3.
- **What it allows:** Relocate to run your U.S. entity; build toward a green card
  without the lottery.
- **What it does not allow / caveats:** Requires a *genuine* foreign business with
  ≥1 year of qualifying employment and a real corporate relationship; "new office"
  L-1 starts at **1 year** and must show growth to extend. Governance/structure
  must be airtight.
- **Real-world use:** The classic **Indian SaaS playbook** — many India-born
  companies (Freshworks, Chargebee, Postman, etc.) were built from India with U.S.
  entities and founder/exec relocations; the L-1→EB-1C route is the standard tool.
- **Sources:** [USCIS — L-1A Intracompany Transferee](https://www.uscis.gov/working-in-the-united-states/temporary-workers/l-1a-intracompany-transferee-executive-or-manager) *(primary)* · [Davies & Associates — L-1 new office guide](https://www.usimmigrationadvisor.com/l1-visa-new-office) *(secondary)*

### C. **EB-2 NIW** — self-petitioned green card (no employer, no PERM, no investment)

- **The hack:** Skip employer sponsorship entirely. With an advanced degree (or
  exceptional ability), self-petition a green card under the **National Interest
  Waiver**, satisfying the **Dhanasar** 3-prong test (substantial merit + national
  importance; well-positioned to advance it; benefits the U.S. to waive PERM).
- **What it allows:** A *self-sponsored green card* for founders building things of
  "national importance" (AI, critical tech, etc.) — no job offer required.
- **What it does not allow / caveats:** Still subject to the **EB-2 India backlog**
  (so slower than EB-1A for priority-date movement), and **tighter in 2026** under
  updated Dhanasar guidance — needs disciplined case-building.
- **Real-world use:** Widely used by founder-researchers; pairs well with an O-1A
  for the work-authorization piece while the green card is pending.
- **Sources:** [USCIS — Immigrant Pathways for Entrepreneurs](https://www.uscis.gov/working-in-the-united-states/entrepreneur-employment-pathways/immigrant-pathways-for-entrepreneur-employment-in-the-united-states) *(primary)* · [Deel — EB-2 NIW for startup founders](https://www.deel.com/blog/the-eb2-niw-for-startup-founders/) *(secondary)*

### D. **EB-5 / regional center** — buy the green card, de-risk the immigration

- **The hack:** If you have liquidity (e.g., a prior exit), invest **$800K+** (TEA)
  or **$1.05M** in a U.S. enterprise (direct or via a **regional center**) creating
  10 jobs → green card for you and family, **decoupled from your own startup's
  success**. India-specific **set-aside** categories can be materially faster.
- **What it allows:** Permanent residency without tying your status to your startup
  surviving; freedom to found anything afterward.
- **What it does not allow / caveats:** Large capital at risk; job-creation and
  source-of-funds scrutiny; not realistic pre-exit for most founders.
- **Real-world use:** Documented among Chennai/Bay-Area serial entrepreneurs who
  explicitly wanted immigration *not* tied to operating a specific business.
- **Sources:** [USCIS — EB-5 Immigrant Investor Program](https://www.uscis.gov/working-in-the-united-states/permanent-workers/eb-5-immigrant-investor-program) *(primary)* · [Global Immigration — EB-5 Indian entrepreneur case study](https://globalimmigration.com/success-stories-immigration-case-studies/) *(secondary)*

### E. **Cap-exempt / concurrent H-1B via a university or nonprofit**

- **The hack:** H-1Bs sponsored by **institutions of higher education or affiliated
  nonprofits** are **cap-exempt** (no lottery). Hold a cap-exempt H-1B (e.g., a
  part-time university/nonprofit role) and run a **concurrent** H-1B for your
  startup — keeping work authorization without depending on the cap lottery.
- **What it allows:** Lottery-free H-1B status plus the ability to add a concurrent
  startup H-1B; a stable base while you build.
- **What it does not allow / caveats:** Requires a real cap-exempt employer/role;
  the concurrent startup petition still needs a bona fide employer-employee setup.
- **Real-world use:** A known structuring tactic among founder-academics and those
  near universities; commonly discussed by startup-immigration counsel.
- **Sources:** [Chugh — Can H-1B/H-4 holders start a business?](https://www.chugh.com/news/h-1b-visas-and-entrepreneurship-can-h-1b-and-h-4-visa-holders-start-a-business) *(secondary)* · [Alma — best visa options for YC founders](https://www.tryalma.com/learn/best-visa-options-yc-company-founders) *(secondary)*

### Most out-of-box options realistically relevant to Clinton

Given Clinton is **Indian, on H-1B, with PERM approved** (no exit liquidity
assumed), the highest-leverage *non-traditional* moves are:

1. **EB-1A self-petition** (à la Arjita Sethi) — the cleanest backlog escape, if
   he builds the record. His approved PERM is a starting data point, not enough alone.
2. **Build-in-public for O-1A/EB-1A** (à la Swizec) — start *now*: content,
   open source, talks, a product with users/press. Evidence compounds over 1–2 years.
3. **EB-2 NIW** — a self-petitioned green card if EB-1A feels out of reach yet,
   though still in the EB-2 India queue (pair with O-1A for work authorization).
4. **Cap-exempt concurrent H-1B** — a lottery-free way to keep status while founding,
   *if* a university/nonprofit affiliation is feasible.

Lower-fit for his profile (but worth knowing): **E-2 via second passport** (large
capital + not a green card), **L-1 flip** (needs a genuine ≥1-yr company abroad),
and **EB-5** (needs ~$800K). These shine *after* an exit or with outside capital.
