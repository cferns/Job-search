# Founder Visa Case Studies — Immigrant Founder Pathways

> **This is research context, not legal advice.** Immigration outcomes are
> highly fact-specific. Verify everything below with a licensed U.S.
> immigration attorney before acting.

A concise research note on immigrant founders who used, or publicly discussed,
non-green-card-first routes to founding a company in the U.S. — H-1B founder
structures, O-1A, concurrent/self-sponsored H-1B, and similar pathways.

Each profile separates **confirmed facts** (supported by cited sources) from
**reasonable interpretation** and **what is unclear**. Where a fact is not
directly stated by a source, it is labeled "not confirmed" or "unclear."

## Jyoti Bansal — AppDynamics

### Snapshot

- **Country of origin:** India
- **Company:** AppDynamics (application performance monitoring)
- **Founded year:** 2008
- **Role:** Founder & CEO
- **Visa pathway mentioned publicly:** Came to the U.S. on an H-1B (~2000);
  publicly stated he waited ~7 years for a green card and founded the company
  *after* receiving permanent residency.
- **Did they wait for green card before founding?** **Yes** (publicly
  documented — the central theme of his immigration story).
- **Relevance to Clinton's situation:** The canonical "green-card-first"
  cautionary tale. Bansal is the most-cited example of an Indian H-1B holder
  who felt he *could not* start a company until his status was resolved.

### Timeline

- **Year: ~2000** — Immigration/career: Moved to the U.S. on an H-1B at age ~21
  to work at a Silicon Valley startup (reported).
- **Year: ~2000–2007** — Immigration: ~7-year wait for a green card; publicly
  described frustration at being unable to start a company while on H-1B.
- **Year: ~2007–2008** — Immigration: Received green card; reportedly quit his
  job "even before the final paperwork came through."
- **Year: 2008** — Startup: Founded AppDynamics.
- **Year: 2017** — Startup: Cisco acquired AppDynamics for ~$3.7B (reported,
  just before its planned IPO).

### What is publicly known

- He immigrated on an H-1B around 2000 and built his career in Silicon Valley
  (publicly documented in multiple interviews).
- He has repeatedly framed the ~7-year green-card wait as the reason he delayed
  founding a company, stating that on a work visa he was not permitted to start
  his own venture and that he founded AppDynamics once he had a green card
  (reported across multiple secondary profiles).
- AppDynamics was founded in 2008 and acquired by Cisco for ~$3.7B in 2017
  (widely reported).
- He has since become a serial founder/investor (e.g., Harness, Traceable,
  Unusual Ventures) and an outspoken commentator on high-skilled immigration.

### What is unclear

- **Exact visa status at founding:** Reported as green card / permanent
  resident, but the precise category (EB-2/EB-3) and exact approval date are
  not confirmed in primary sources reviewed here.
- **Whether his then-employer sponsored him:** Presumed employer-sponsored
  green card, but not confirmed in the sources here.
- **Exact transition year:** The "~7 years" and 2007–2008 window are reported
  approximations, not precisely documented dates.
- Whether he *legally had to* wait vs. *chose* to wait is a matter of
  interpretation. His public framing is that founding on H-1B was not viable
  for him; the modern legal view (see "Lessons" below) is more nuanced.

### Lessons for an Indian H-1B founder

Bansal's story is from the 2000s and predates wider awareness of structures
like concurrent H-1B and founder-as-beneficiary arrangements. The *modern*
takeaway is **not** "you must wait for a green card." It is: (1) on H-1B you
generally cannot simply work for your own company without a valid
employer-employee/sponsorship structure, and (2) the green-card backlog for
Indian nationals can be a decade-plus, so waiting may not be a realistic plan.
His experience illustrates the *cost of waiting*, which motivates exploring the
O-1A / concurrent-H-1B / founder-H-1B routes the other founders below used.

## Practical Paths for Clinton

Context assumed: **Indian national, currently on H-1B, PERM approved**
(I-140 not necessarily filed/approved yet), wants to start an **AI/software**
startup **before** receiving a green card. Indian EB-2/EB-3 priority dates are
backlogged many years, so "just wait for the green card" may mean a decade-plus.

> **Reminder: research context, not legal advice.** "PERM approved" is an early
> milestone — it is *not* an I-140 approval and does not by itself confer any
> ability to work for a new company. Confirm your exact stage with counsel.

### 1. Stay on current H-1B + own equity only

- **What it allows:** You can *own* equity in a startup and be a passive
  shareholder/investor while remaining employed (and sponsored) by your current
  H-1B employer. You can incorporate, hold founder shares, and let the company
  exist.
- **What it does not allow:** You generally **cannot actively work** for or
  perform services for your own startup on the current H-1B (it authorizes work
  only for the sponsoring employer in the approved role). No operational/CEO
  work, no "moonlighting" for the new entity.
- **When it becomes realistic:** Immediately — but only as a *passive owner*
  while a co-founder/team runs operations.
- **Risk level:** **Low** (if truly passive and well-documented) — but easy to
  cross the line into unauthorized work.
- **Attorney questions to ask:** What exactly counts as "passive" ownership vs.
  prohibited "work"? Can I be a board member, sign incorporation docs, or make
  high-level decisions? How do I keep my primary H-1B clean while the company
  exists?

### 2. Concurrent H-1B through the startup

- **What it allows:** A *second*, concurrent H-1B filed by the startup (or a
  friendly entity) lets you work for both your current employer **and** the
  startup at the same time, each part-time, on cap-exempt concurrent petitions
  (you already hold an H-1B, so no new lottery needed).
- **What it does not allow:** The startup petition still needs a valid
  employer-employee relationship and a real role/wage; you can't simply "pay
  yourself nothing." It doesn't remove your dependence on maintaining the
  primary H-1B.
- **When it becomes realistic:** Once the startup is a real entity that can act
  as an employer (EIN, ability to pay required wage, governance to supervise
  you). Often feasible relatively early.
- **Risk level:** **Medium** — structurally sound and common, but documentation
  and the employer-employee relationship must be solid.
- **Attorney questions to ask:** Can my startup file a concurrent H-1B for me
  now? What governance (independent board / control) do I need so the company
  can lawfully "employ" me? What's the minimum viable wage/role? What happens to
  the concurrent H-1B if my primary H-1B ends?

### 3. Founder H-1B / beneficiary-owner route (Sid Sarasvati-style)

- **What it allows:** Under the 2025 H-1B modernization rules, a founder who
  **owns a controlling interest** can be the H-1B *beneficiary* of their own
  company if there's a bona fide employer-employee relationship (e.g., an
  independent board with authority to hire/fire/supervise the beneficiary).
  Your startup sponsors *you*. (Initial/extension validity for owner-founders
  may be limited to shorter increments.)
- **What it does not allow:** You can't be the sole, unchecked controller with
  no oversight structure; the relationship must be genuine. It's more legally
  delicate than a standard third-party H-1B. A *receipt* is not an approval.
- **When it becomes realistic:** Once you've built the corporate governance
  (board/control terms) and the company can pay the required wage. Often after
  some traction/funding.
- **Risk level:** **Medium-High** — newer, scrutinized structure; viable but
  must be set up precisely.
- **Attorney questions to ask:** Does my cap structure/board satisfy the
  beneficiary-owner employer-employee test? What validity period should I
  expect? How does this interact with my existing PERM/priority date? What are
  the renewal risks?

### 4. O-1A founder route (Arko / Soundarya / Aditya-style)

- **What it allows:** O-1A ("extraordinary ability") lets you work for your own
  venture (via a U.S. petitioner/agent) without the H-1B lottery or a green
  card. No annual cap; renewable. Lets you run the company actively.
- **What it does not allow:** It's not a green card and is tied to qualifying
  activity; you must *meet the extraordinary-ability bar* (multiple criteria:
  press, awards, funding, judging, memberships, original contributions). It
  needs a petitioner/agent structure.
- **When it becomes realistic:** Once you've **built and documented** a
  qualifying record. Your **approved PERM** (and any future I-140) can be *one*
  supporting data point. Realistic in months-to-a-year-plus of deliberate
  record-building.
- **Risk level:** **Medium** — well-trodden founder path, but approval depends
  on evidence quality.
- **Attorney questions to ask:** Given my record, which O-1A criteria do I
  plausibly meet today, and what gaps should I close? Who serves as my
  petitioner/agent as a founder? Can I move H-1B → O-1A without leaving the
  U.S.? Does pursuing O-1A jeopardize my existing PERM/green-card track?

### 5. EB-1A long-term route (the green-card version of O-1A)

- **What it allows:** EB-1A is a *self-petitioned green card* for extraordinary
  ability — **no employer sponsor and no PERM needed**, and (critically for
  Indian nationals) it can have a **much shorter backlog** than EB-2/EB-3.
  Aditya Sharma's documented path: O-1 to operate, EB-1A for the green card.
- **What it does not allow:** It's a high bar (similar criteria to O-1A but
  judged more strictly) and doesn't itself authorize work while pending (you
  still need an underlying status like H-1B/O-1).
- **When it becomes realistic:** After building a strong, *sustained* record —
  typically the medium-to-long term, often layered *on top of* an O-1A you got
  first.
- **Risk level:** **Medium-High** (on evidence/approval) but **strategically
  high-value** because it can leapfrog the EB-2/EB-3 India backlog.
- **Attorney questions to ask:** Is EB-1A realistic for me and on what timeline?
  Should I file EB-1A while keeping my existing EB-2/EB-3 (PERM) case alive for
  priority-date retention? Can I "port" my current priority date to an EB-1A
  I-140? O-1A first, then EB-1A — right sequence for me?

### 6. Wait for I-140 / EAD / green card path (the Jyoti-Bansal default)

- **What it allows:** Stay on H-1B with your employer, let PERM → I-140 →
  (eventually) I-485/EAD play out. With an approved I-140 you get H-1B
  extensions beyond 6 years; once you can file I-485, you'd get an **EAD** that
  *does* let you work for your own company.
- **What it does not allow:** Until you reach the I-485/EAD stage, you **cannot
  actively work** for your own startup. For Indian EB-2/EB-3, reaching I-485 can
  take **many years** due to backlogs — so the EAD that unlocks founding may be
  far away.
- **When it becomes realistic:** EAD eligibility depends on your priority date
  becoming "current" (or being filed under any available window) — potentially
  a decade-plus for India EB-2/EB-3. I-140 approval (sooner) only buys H-1B
  extensions, not the right to work for the startup.
- **Risk level:** **Low immigration risk**, but **high opportunity cost** —
  this is precisely the "waited 7 years" scenario that delayed Jyoti Bansal.
- **Attorney questions to ask:** What's my realistic I-485/EAD timeline given my
  category and priority date? Does an approved I-140 let me do *anything* with my
  own company before I-485? Can I run an O-1A/EB-1A track *in parallel* without
  losing my current priority date?

### Quick synthesis for Clinton

- **Want to start building now, passively:** Path 1 (own equity, co-founder
  runs ops).
- **Want to actively work on the startup soonest while keeping H-1B:** Paths 2
  or 3 (concurrent / founder H-1B).
- **Want to fully run the company on your own status:** Path 4 (O-1A) — the
  route all three *modern* founders above used or discussed.
- **Want to escape the India backlog for the green card itself:** Path 5
  (EB-1A), ideally layered on an O-1A.
- **Lowest effort, highest opportunity cost:** Path 6 (wait) — the dated
  default; rarely the best plan for an Indian national given backlog length.

These paths are **not mutually exclusive** — a common strategy is *concurrent/
founder H-1B or O-1A to build now* **+** *EB-1A in parallel to fix the green
card*, while keeping the existing PERM/EB-2 case alive for priority-date
retention.
